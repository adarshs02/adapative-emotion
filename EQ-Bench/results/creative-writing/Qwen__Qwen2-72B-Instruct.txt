In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. #####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The bar was dim, the air thick with smoke and the scent of stale beer. The soldier sat alone, his elbows resting on the worn oak counter. He wore a three-day stubble and a fixed stare. His left hand, missing two fingers, drummed a rhythmless beat on the scarred wood. He wore his fatigue like a coat, draped over his shoulders.

The bartender, a man with swollen eyes and a gray mustache, set a glass in front of him. "Whiskey," he said, no question in his voice. 

The soldier nodded, no smile on his face, no thank you on his lips. He sipped the whiskey, the burn sliding down his throat, a familiar friend. 

A man walked in, loud and boisterous, the type who thought his money could buy him respect. He wore a suit too tight, his belly straining the buttons. He caught sight of the soldier and his missing fingers. 

"What happened to your hand?" he asked, too loud, too curious.

The soldier looked at him, his eyes flat. "War," he said, and nothing more.

The man laughed, a hollow sound. "Must have been a hell of a fight," he said, clapping the soldier on the back, a little too hard.

The soldier winced, but didn't show it. "Something like that," he said, his voice as flat as his eyes.

The man laughed again, louder this time. "Bet you've got some stories," he said, pulling up a stool.

The soldier looked at him, really looked at him, and saw nothing but a man who'd never known real fear, real loss. "Stories," he said, his voice dripping with disdain. "I've got a million of them. Want to hear one?"

The man nodded, his eyes eager, his beer forgotten.

The soldier leaned in, his voice low. "Once, in a place far away, a man lost his friends, his innocence, and two fingers. He came home, and no one cared. The end."

The man laughed, not hearing the truth in the soldier's words. "That's it?" he said, disappointed.

The soldier nodded, his eyes never leaving the man's face. "That's it," he said, his voice as empty as the man's laughter. 

The man shrugged, his interest gone. "Not much of a story," he said, and walked away, leaving the soldier alone with his whiskey and his memories.

The soldier watched him go, his eyes following the man's retreating form. He finished his whiskey, the burn a welcome distraction. He signaled the bartender for another, his eyes never leaving the scene before him.

The bartender nodded, his eyes understanding. He poured the soldier another whiskey, no words needed between them. They were both men who had seen too much, felt too much, and knew the truth of the soldier's story.

The soldier drank his whiskey, the burn easing the pain of his memories. He looked at his missing fingers, the scars a constant reminder of the war he'd left behind. He smiled, a wry, humorless smile. He knew the truth of his story, and that was enough for him.

The bar was still dim, the air still thick with smoke and the scent of stale beer. But the soldier didn't mind. He was home, and that was all that mattered.
