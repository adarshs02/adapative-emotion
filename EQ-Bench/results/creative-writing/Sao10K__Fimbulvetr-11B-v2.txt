Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

*Wednesday*

Another blasted day of solitude. Still, the forest does not fail to provide. I'm sure the ruddy sun is shining, but it doesn't reach me here in these suffocating depths. I've grown accustomed to the darkness, though. It's comforting in its own way.

*Friday*

There they were, those two rascals. Lost and hungry, as if the world owes them sustenance. I could see the fear in their eyes, like two frightened doe caught in headlights. My heart softened for them. How they reminded me of my own youth.

"Come in, children," I called, my voice as sweet as honey. "You're safe here."

"Aren't you going to eat us?" the boy dared to ask, his voice small and quivering.

How could they think such a thing? "Of course not," I laughed, the sound echoing through the cottage. "I'm not a monster."

*Saturday*

The boy, Hansel, seemed wary, while the girl, Gretel, was more trusting. I don't blame the lad; suspicion runs deep in those who've known nothing but hardship. The poor things are filthy, but I've no choice but to clean them up. I can see the potential for beauty in their ragged forms.

Their clothes are tattered beyond repair, but I have a loom, and I can weave new ones for them. I'll make sure they're well-fed, too. No one deserves to live in hunger.

*Sunday*

Hansel's eyes widen as he sees the table laden with food, as if he can't believe his luck. Gretel, however, is more cautious. "Is this all for us?" she asks, her voice full of hope. "Yes, little one, it's all for you," I reply, watching as they devour the feast.

They're so eager, it's heartbreaking. My heart aches for the childhoods they never had, for all the laughter they've missed, all the love they've been denied.

*Monday*

I'm teaching Gretel to weave. I've always found solace in the rhythm of the loom and think it'll do her good. Hansel, on the other hand, seems more interested in exploring the house. He's been peering into my books, so I've shown him how to read.

I wish I could say I found joy in his eagerness, but there's a nagging voice in the back of my mind. They're too trusting. If they're not mine by blood, they're mine by deed. Right?

*Tuesday*

Hansel has discovered the room full of sweets. He stuffs his mouth, not caring for the stickiness or the crumbs that litter his face. "You'll burst!" I tease, but he just grins around his mouthful.

Gretel, however, is nowhere to be found. I've searched high and low, yet she's vanished without a trace. Where could she have gone?

*Wednesday*

Found her. She's locked herself in the oven, of all places. I had to gently coax her out. She seems to think I'm planning to bake her. "I would never hurt you," I tell her, cupping her face in my hands.

She stares at me, her eyes so full of wariness. It breaks my heart.

*Thursday*

Hansel's gone, too. I suppose I should have seen it coming. He was always the more defiant of the two. The boy's left a trail of breadcrumbs to find his way back to the real world, as if I'd ever try to keep him.

It's Gretel who's my concern now. I've to win her trust before she, too, runs off.

*Friday*

I've set up a little dollhouse for Gretel. Watching her play with it brings a warmth to my chest. She's still wary, but her smiles are more frequent, and her laughs are louder. She's thriving.

I can't help but wonder if I'm doing the right thing, keeping her here, away from the cruel world. She's safe, happy, and loved. Isn't that what matters?

*Saturday*

Gretel's declared we're going to be a family. It's an odd thought, but it fits. We're bound by love and care, if not by blood.

But there's a heaviness in my chest, a knowledge that this cannot last. People will come looking for the children, and when they do, they'll see the worst in me. The tale of the wicked witch seeking to devour innocents.

*Sunday*

They've come. A man and a woman, frantic and desperate, blaming me. I'm accused of kidnapping, held at bay with torches and pitchforks.

Gretel's by my side, her small form a stark reminder of the innocence I've nurtured. I want to tell them how I've only tried to do what's right, how I've given them comfort, security, love.

Instead, I watch as the cottage burns, a testament to the misunderstanding. I hold Gretel tight, the heat licking at our heels.

We'll survive. I have to. For her, for the family we've built, for the hope we've found. The world may see me as a villain, but she, and I, know the truth.

I hurried back to the barracks, the wet toga clinging to my body. I knelt before my cot, my thoughts turning to my own immortality. In the gladiator's life, death was a constant companion, a shadow that grew ever closer with each passing day. I closed my eyes and whispered a prayer to Bacchus, asking for the gift of one more day—a day of survival, so that I might know just a moment longer the intoxicating embrace of life within the confines of this cruel yet wondrous empire.

Epistolary Narrative: Write a story told entirely through a series of brief correspondences: telegrams or letters or emails between two characters. Dear Mother,

The lighthouse is a grand sight, with its gleaming beacon reaching for the skies. It's a fine post, no doubt about that. I've settled in and the keeper's cottage is cozy enough. Your loving son,

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
Nick walked into the dimly lit bar, the door banging shut behind him. A few heads turned, their gaze lingering on the medals pinned to his chest. He scanned the room, finding an empty stool in the corner. He took off his hat, revealing a scar that ran from his eyebrow to his temple. He slapped it onto the bar, sat down, and ordered a beer.

The bartender, an old man with calloused hands, slid the bottle to him without a word. Nick took a long pull, then wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. The place filled up, the chatter growing louder. A group of men at the other end of the bar started laughing, slapping each other on the back.

"Getting a late start?" Nick asked, nodding at the group.

The old bartender snorted. "Work's tough on them. Never seen a day like you have."

Nick grunted. "Work," he said, taking another drink. "Ain't that the word for it."

One of the men from the group, a young fellow with a shiny new hat, walked over. "You a soldier?" he asked.

"Used to be," Nick answered, sizing him up.

The young man's eyes widened. "Hell of a war. Heard there was a lot of bloodshed."

"Lot of that," Nick agreed, nodding.

The young man looked at Nick's scar, his eyes narrowing. "Got that over there, huh?"

"Yeah," Nick said, running his fingers over it. "You going?"

The young man shrugged. "Not sure yet. Don't want to leave my wife and kid."

"Smart man," Nick said. "Wives and kids are a hell of a thing."

A woman walked into the bar, her eyes scanning the room. She caught sight of Nick, her face lighting up. "Nick!" she called, waving.

Nick returned the wave, watching her approach. Her name was Anna, a nurse from back home. She was the only one who knew the real Nick, the one who saw the nightmares in his eyes. She slid onto the stool next to him, her hand on his.

"Nick," she said, her voice soft, "you look tired."

"I am, Anna," he replied, squeezing her hand.

The young man, curious, walked over. "This is my woman, Anna," Nick said.

The young man nodded, then excused himself to rejoin his friends.

Anna leaned in, her voice low. "Tell me about the war, Nick. I've been waiting."

Nick took a long drink, his eyes distant. "There's not much to tell, Anna. Just a lot of blood, a lot of death. Men screaming, crying, praying. The stench, the mud, the cold." He paused, his voice hoarse. "Sometimes we'd just sit, waiting, not knowing when it would come again."

Anna held his hand tighter. "And you made it through it all."

He glanced at her, a wry smile playing on his lips. "I guess I did. Now all I want is to forget."

The bar grew louder, the chatter blending into a cacophony of voices. Nick drained his beer, then ordered another. The scenes of war played in his mind, the carnage and destruction, a constant loop.

But there was Anna, her hand in his, a reminder of home, of peace. He looked at her, at the tenderness in her eyes, and knew he could tell her anything. He squeezed her hand, grateful for the moment of respite in the midst of the chaos.

The night wore on, the bar filling and emptying like the tides. Nick sat there, a silent sentinel, a man who had seen the worst humanity had to offer. But through it all, he held onto Anna, a beacon of hope in the midst of the darkness.

As the last call came, Nick stood, his hat back on his head. Anna stood with him, her arm around his waist. They walked out into the night, the war behind them, for now. They made their way home, into each other's arms, finding solace in the comfort of the life they had, the life they would rebuild.

And so, Nick returned to the world he had left, a changed man, a man with stories untold, with scars that would never fade. But he faced the future with Anna by his side, a man now searching for the small moments of peace that would eventually mend the wounds of war.
