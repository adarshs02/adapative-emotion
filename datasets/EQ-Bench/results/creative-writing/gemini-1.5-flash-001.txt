Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

## The Witch's Book

**Day 1**

They came, the little ones. Lost, starving, eyes hollow. The woods, they whisper, are cruel. I know. I know their hunger. I know their fear. I offered them bread, sweet and warm. They ate, their faces brightening. They are mine now.

**Day 2**

Hansel, the boy, is strong. He tries to be brave, but his eyes betray him. Gretel, the girl, is clever. She watches, always watching. She sees the truth. 

"Why do you keep us here, lady?" she asks, voice small but sharp.

"I am not a lady, child. I am a woman who loves to cook," I say, my voice gentle. "You are my guests. Guests need to be fed."

**Day 3**

They ask about their parents. Their voices break with each question. I tell them stories of the woods, of the dangers that lurk within. I tell them stories of the witch who lives in the forest, a wicked one who lures children with sweet promises. I tell them stories of a woman who rescues children from the wicked witch. 

"But you are the witch," Hansel whispers, eyes wide with fear.

"I am no witch," I say, my voice rising. "I am a woman who needs help. The witch has stolen my happiness, my family, my home. She is the one who needs to be stopped."

**Day 4**

They believe me. I see it in their eyes. They trust me. They are mine now. 

"We will help you," Gretel says, her voice firm.

"You will," I whisper, my heart swelling with a warmth I haven't felt in years.

**Day 5**

They help with the chores. They gather firewood, clean the house, bake bread. They are strong, these children. They are good. They are mine now.

**Day 10**

I see the fear in their eyes again. They are growing thin, their skin pale. I tell them it is the witch's curse, the poison she has spread through the woods. I tell them they need to be stronger, to resist the curse. 

"We need to leave," Hansel says, voice trembling.

"Leave? Where would you go?" I ask, my voice sharp. "The woods are full of danger. The witch is everywhere. You are safe here."

**Day 20**

They are weaker now. Their eyes are dull, their bodies fragile. I tell them it is the witch's doing, the curse she has cast upon them. I tell them I must make them stronger, to fight the curse. 

"You are hurting us," Gretel whispers, tears in her eyes.

"I am helping you," I say, my voice firm. "I am saving you."

**Day 30**

They are quiet now. They barely move. They are mine now.

**Day 40**

The oven is hot. I tell them it is for the witch, to lure her out. I tell them it is for their safety, to protect them from the curse.

"We don't want to go in the oven," Hansel whispers, his voice barely audible.

"It will be okay," I say, my voice soft. "You will be safe."

**Day 41**

They are gone. The oven is cold. The house is empty. The woods are silent. 

I am alone. 

I am a woman who loves to cook.

I am a woman who needs help.

I am a woman who is misunderstood. 

I am a woman who is alone.

I was a gladiator, a warrior, a son of Mars. But I was also a man, a son of Rome, a son of the gods. In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. The bar was dim, the air thick with smoke and the smell of stale beer. It was the kind of place where the shadows seemed to hold their breath, waiting for something to happen. The soldier sat at the counter, his back to the wall, a half-empty glass of whiskey in front of him. He was a tall man, with a face that had seen too much. His eyes were the color of old, faded denim, and his lips were thin and tight, like they were holding back a story he didn't want to tell.

"Another?" The bartender, a man with a face like a worn leather boot, leaned in. 

The soldier nodded, his gaze fixed on the empty glass. 

"You look like you've seen a ghost," the bartender said. 

The soldier shrugged. "Maybe I have."

A woman sat down next to him. She was dressed in a bright red dress, the kind of dress that screamed for attention. She smelled of perfume and cheap cigarettes.

"You're in the army, aren't you?" she asked. "I can tell."

The soldier looked away. He didn't want to talk about it. He didn't want to explain. 

"What's your name?" she asked.

"Jack."

"Jack," she said, her voice soft. "Jack, what are you waiting for?"

"I don't know," he said, his voice rough. "I guess I'm waiting for something to happen."

"Well, it's happening now," she said, her hand reaching out to touch his arm.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I'm not in the mood."

She shrugged. "Suit yourself," she said. "I'll find someone else."

She stood up and walked away, her red dress a flash of color in the dimness.

He finished his drink and stood up. He walked out of the bar, the night air cool on his face. He didn't know where he was going, but he knew he needed to get away. He needed to find a place where he could be alone, where he could try to forget.

He walked until he reached the river. He sat down on the bank, his feet dangling in the cold water. He closed his eyes and listened to the sound of the water flowing, the sound of the city in the distance. 

He was a soldier. He was a man. And he was lost.
