# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
In the dim light of the bar, the soldier sat alone, nursing a drink. He was a tall man, broad-shouldered and lean, with a face that had seen too much sun and wind. His hair was cut short, military style, and there was a scar above his left eyebrow, pale against his tanned skin. He wore a simple shirt and trousers, but the way he held himself, the way his eyes moved, told of his time in the war.

The bar was quiet, save for the low hum of conversation and the clink of glasses. The soldier stared at his drink, a whiskey, neat. He'd always preferred it that way, ever since his first tour. It reminded him of the campfires, the camaraderie, the shared stories of home. He took a sip, felt the burn at the back of his throat, and let out a sigh.

"Another one, soldier?" The bartender, a grizzled old man with a kind smile, nodded towards his glass.

"Sure, why not?" The soldier replied, his voice rough, worn from years of shouting orders and barking commands.

As the bartender poured another drink, the soldier's eyes wandered around the bar. Posters of old war heroes lined the walls, their faces stern and resolute. He recognized them all, had studied them in his youth, dreamed of being like them. Now, he just felt tired.

"You know, I was in the war too," the bartender said, placing the fresh drink in front of the soldier. "Korea, '52. Toughest year of my life."

The soldier nodded, taking a sip of his whiskey. "I bet," he said, his voice barely above a whisper.

The bartender leaned in, his eyes filled with a knowing look. "But you know what got me through?" he asked, a small smile playing on his lips. "Humour. You gotta find the humour in the madness, soldier. That's what keeps you sane."

The soldier let out a small chuckle, almost against his will. "I guess you're right," he said, his eyes meeting the bartender's. "I remember this one time, we were pinned down by enemy fire. Bullets flying everywhere. And then, out of nowhere, one of my men starts singing. 'I've got a lovely bunch of coconuts,' he sang, as loud as he could. Made us all laugh. Even the enemy, I think."

The bartender let out a hearty laugh, slapping the counter. "That's the spirit, soldier!" he said, his eyes twinkling. "You gotta find the funny in the chaos."

The soldier took another sip of his drink, feeling the warmth spread through his chest. He looked around the bar, at the people lost in their own worlds, their own problems. He thought about the war, the battles, the losses. And despite it all, he felt a small smile tug at the corners of his lips.

"You're right," he said, meeting the bartender's gaze. "You're absolutely right."

As the night wore on, the soldier found himself sharing more stories, more laughs. The bartender listened, nodded, laughed along. And in that small, dimly lit bar, the soldier found a sense of peace he hadn't felt in a long time.

When he finally left, the sun was just starting to rise, painting the sky with shades of orange and pink. He took a deep breath, filling his lungs with the cool morning air. He looked back at the bar, at the old man still behind the counter, and nodded.

"Thanks," he said, his voice barely a whisper. "For everything."

The bartender just smiled, raising his hand in a small salute. "Anytime, soldier. Anytime."

And with that, the soldier turned and walked away, leaving the bar and its memories behind. He had a long journey ahead, a journey back to the world he had left behind. But he knew he would be alright. He had found his humour in the madness. And that, he knew, was worth more than any medal or honour.
